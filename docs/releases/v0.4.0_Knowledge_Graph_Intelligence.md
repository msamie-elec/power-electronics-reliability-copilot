# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 Purpose

This document defines the engineering ontology for the Power Electronics Reliability Copilot.

The ontology provides the conceptual foundation for representing engineering knowledge inside a Neo4j knowledge graph. It defines the entities, relationships, properties and modelling principles required to support explainable engineering reasoning, GraphRAG, and future AI agent workflows.

Rather than treating engineering documents as isolated pieces of text, the ontology enables engineering concepts to be represented as connected knowledge that can be searched, traversed and reasoned over.

---

## 1.2 Scope

Version 1 of the ontology focuses on reliability analysis of IGBT power modules.

The ontology has intentionally been kept small to establish a robust and extensible graph structure before expanding into broader power electronics systems.

Current scope includes:

- IGBT Modules
- Bond Wires
- Solder Layers
- Thermal Cycling
- Junction Temperature
- Failure Mechanisms
- Failure Modes
- Symptoms
- Diagnostic Tests
- Maintenance Actions
- Engineering Document Evidence

---

## 1.3 Design Principles

The ontology follows six fundamental principles.

### Principle 1 — Engineering concepts should be explicit

Important engineering concepts should exist as graph nodes rather than text embedded inside documents.

Example:

```

Thermal Cycling

Bond Wire Fatigue

Junction Temperature

```

---

### Principle 2 — Relationships explain engineering knowledge

Relationships represent engineering logic rather than database connections.

Example:

```

Thermal Cycling
CAUSES
Bond Wire Fatigue

```

---

### Principle 3 — Engineering evidence must be traceable

Every engineering recommendation should be linked to its supporting document evidence.

This enables explainable AI.

---

### Principle 4 — Reuse concepts whenever possible

The same engineering concept should be represented once and reused throughout the graph.

For example:

```

Thermal Cycling

```

may contribute to

- Bond Wire Fatigue

- Solder Fatigue

- TIM Degradation

---

### Principle 5 — Grow incrementally

The ontology starts with one engineering domain and expands gradually.

---

### Principle 6 — Graph-first design

The ontology is designed primarily for graph traversal rather than relational storage.

# 8. Design Decisions

This section documents architectural decisions.

## Why are failure mechanisms nodes?

Failure mechanisms participate in many engineering relationships.

Representing them as nodes allows multiple failure modes to reference the same mechanism.

---

## Why is document evidence represented separately?

GraphRAG requires retrieved evidence to remain connected to engineering concepts.

---

## Why are relationship properties used?

Relationships contain engineering confidence, evidence strength and retrieval metadata.

---

## Why is the ontology centred on IGBT modules?

IGBT modules provide a realistic yet manageable engineering domain for the first knowledge graph.

# My recommendation

Before inserting **any data** into Neo4j, we should design the **database schema**.

This is exactly how enterprise teams work.

There are three layers:

```text
Ontology
      │
      ▼
Graph Schema
      │
      ▼
Graph Data
```

People often jump directly from ontology to creating nodes.

I don't recommend that.

# This is the approach I recommend

Rather than inventing dozens of engineering entities from memory, let's build the graph from **real engineering literature**. We can use authoritative sources such as application notes, textbooks, and reliability papers so every node and relationship is evidence-based.

The workflow would be:

1. Select one engineering domain (I'd start with **IGBT power modules**).
2. Extract the key entities (components, mechanisms, failure modes, symptoms, tests, maintenance actions).
3. Create the corresponding Neo4j nodes.
4. Link them with relationships.
5. Cite the source document that justified each part of the graph.

This has three advantages:

* Your graph becomes technically credible.
* It naturally grows as you add more documents.
* Later, when you publish or demonstrate the project, you can show that the knowledge graph is grounded in engineering evidence rather than manually invented.

I think this is the point where your project starts to become more than a coding exercise—it becomes a genuine AI engineering knowledge platform.
